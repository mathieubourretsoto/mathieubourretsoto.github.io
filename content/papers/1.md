##### Abstract

This study examines the lasting effects of political repression in Chile during the Pinochet regime (1973-1990) on political attitudes. Chile's political transitions from the socialist government to dictatorship and then to democracy, the human rights violations committed during the dictatorship, and the enduring legacy of the Pinochet era make it an ideal setting to study the long-term effects of political repression. Employing a difference-in-differences approach, this study combined survey data from the Social Longitudinal Survey of Chile (ELSOC) and political repression data from Bautista et al. (2023) and Freire et al. (2017) to explore the impact of exposure to political repression during adolescence and young adulthood on current political attitudes. This study contributes to the literature on the long-term consequences of political repression by testing the impressionable year hypothesis, highlighting the importance of age when enduring political repression on future political outcomes, and incorporating geolocated variables. The findings suggest that exposure to political violence during the critical period (i.e., 17-25 years old) has a significant positive impact on current political trust in the government, the president, and political engagement, compared to exposure at a lower age. Additionally, the estimates revealed a negative effect on long-term trust in the Carabineros. Additional analyses were performed throughout the paper to check the sensitivity of the results and explore potential mechanisms that may drive them. Overall, I present evidence that the detrimental effects of political repression in the former dictatorship may persist even several decades later.
