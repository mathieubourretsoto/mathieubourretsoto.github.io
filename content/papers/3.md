##### Abstract

Global warming will lead to more frequent and intense heatwaves across all regions of the world in the next years and decades. Adaptation at the individual level plays a key role in order to help populations cope with these extreme events. The present paper undertakes a systematic review with meta-analysis of the available academic literature on the determinants of heat-adaptive behaviors (HABs) following the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic reviews and Meta-Analysis (PRISMA) guideline. A Boolean algorithm was used on two online databases (PubMed and Web of Science Core Collection) in order to retrieve relevant and quantitative peer-reviewed papers. A total of 73 papers met all the inclusion criteria and were included in the systematic review. Two-third of the studies focused on high-income economies (N=48), the rest focusing on middle-income economies. After presenting descriptive results, a meta-analysis was performed using a random-effects model. The emphasis was made on heat-related perceptions (perceived efficacy, perceived risk, and perceived threat), heat-related knowledge and health status (objective or subjective) as these variables are the most likely to provide levers of behavioral change. Thirty-one studies were then included in the meta-analysis. Perceived efficacy, heat-related knowledge and perceived threat are positively associated with the adoption of HABs. Quite unexpectedly, one’s health status does not play a clear role in the adaptation to heat. A moderation analyses was then undertaken to investigate possible sources of heterogeneity between studies (i.e. type of analysis, country income group, and population concerned). This study emphasizes various gaps in the current literature, regarding either the populations studied, the statistical methods used or the lack of standardization of HABs or perception concepts. In the end, we highlight several policy recommendations to limit the adverse effects of heatwaves on populations’ health.
